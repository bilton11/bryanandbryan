Small Claims Court Process Guide

A plain-language guide to the Ontario Small Claims Court process, from before you file through enforcement.

Important: This guide provides general legal information about the Ontario Small Claims Court process. It is not legal advice. For advice about your specific situation, consult a licensed lawyer or paralegal.

{# ========================================================================= ACCORDION SECTIONS — 8 Stages ========================================================================= #} {# --- STAGE 1: Before You File --- #}

Is Small Claims Court right for you?

Ontario Small Claims Court handles civil (non-criminal) disputes worth up to {{ monetary_limit_formatted }}. As of October 2024, this limit was raised from $35,000 to $50,000. The court handles money claims and claims for the return of personal property valued under the limit.

The following types of claims cannot be brought in Small Claims Court:

  • Claims about title to land or real property
  • Bankruptcy matters
  • False imprisonment or malicious prosecution
  • Claims exceeding $50,000 (you can reduce your claim to the limit)

Do you have a case?

Before filing, ask yourself three questions:

  1. What happened? — Can you clearly explain the events that gave rise to your claim?
  2. Who is responsible? — Can you identify the specific person or company who owes you money?
  3. What are your damages? — Can you quantify your losses in dollars?

You also need to be able to prove your case on a balance of probabilities — meaning it is more likely than not that what you say happened actually happened.

Is your claim within the limitation period?

In Ontario, you generally have 2 years from the date you discovered your claim to file it. This is called the basic limitation period under the Limitations Act, 2002.

The "discovery" date is when you first knew (or should have known) that you had a claim. In most cases, this is the date of the incident or the date a debt became due and was not paid.

Important exceptions:

  • Claims involving minors: the 2-year period does not start running until the minor turns 18
  • Claims involving persons who were legally incapable: limitation period may be extended
  • An ultimate limitation period of 15 years applies regardless of discovery

If your claim might be close to the limitation period, consult a lawyer or paralegal immediately.

What evidence do you need?

Evidence is what you will use to prove your claim at trial. Common evidence includes:

  • Contracts, agreements, invoices, receipts
  • Text messages, emails, and letters
  • Photographs of damaged property or incomplete work
  • Bank statements showing payments made
  • Estimates or repair quotes from professionals
  • Witnesses who saw or heard relevant events

Gather and organize your evidence before filing. You do not submit it at the time of filing, but you will need it at the settlement conference and trial.

What will it cost?

Filing fees depend on whether you are a frequent or infrequent claimant:

  • Infrequent claimant (fewer than 10 claims per year): {{ filing_fee_infrequent }} filing fee
  • Frequent claimant (10 or more claims per year): {{ filing_fee_frequent }} filing fee

Additional costs may include: serving the defendant, travel to court, expert witnesses, and representation. If you lose, the court can order you to pay some of the other party's costs.

Consider alternatives first

Before filing, consider whether a less formal approach might resolve your dispute faster and at lower cost:

  • Send a demand letter — formally request payment in writing and give a deadline. Many disputes settle at this stage. Generate a demand letter →
  • Mediation — a neutral mediator helps both parties reach a voluntary settlement
  • Direct negotiation — sometimes a calm phone call or meeting resolves things

Pre-Filing Checklist

  • My claim is for $50,000 or less
  • My claim is within the 2-year limitation period
  • I can identify the defendant's full legal name and address
  • I have evidence that supports my claim
  • I have tried to resolve this informally (demand letter, negotiation)
  • I understand the filing fee and potential costs if I lose
{# --- STAGE 2: Filing the Claim --- #}

Choosing the right court location

You must file in the correct Small Claims Court location. You can file where:

  • The cause of action (the event that gave rise to your claim) arose, OR
  • The defendant lives or carries on business

Filing in the wrong location may result in your claim being transferred, which causes delays.

Completing the Plaintiff's Claim (Form 7A)

The Plaintiff's Claim (Form 7A) is the official court document that starts your case. It includes:

  • Your full name and address (as plaintiff)
  • The defendant's full name and address
  • A clear description of what happened (the "reasons for claim")
  • The amount you are claiming and how you calculated it

Be specific and factual. Use dates, dollar amounts, and specific details. Avoid emotional language — stick to what happened.

Generate your Form 7A using our tool →

Filing options

  • In person: Bring 3 copies of the signed claim to the court office (1 for you, 1 for the court, 1 for each defendant)
  • By mail: Mail to the court office with a cheque or money order for the filing fee
  • Online: Some Ontario Small Claims Courts accept online filing — check ontario.ca/courts

Filing fees

  • {{ filing_fee_infrequent }} — infrequent claimant (fewer than 10 claims per year)
  • {{ filing_fee_frequent }} — frequent claimant (10 or more claims per year)

What happens after you file

The court assigns a claim number to your case. Keep this number — you will use it on all future documents. The court stamps your copies and returns them for service on the defendant.

{# --- STAGE 3: Serving the Defendant --- #}

What is service?

"Service" is the formal legal process of delivering court documents to the defendant. The defendant must be properly served before the court process can move forward. Improper service can get your case dismissed.

Methods of service

Personal service (preferred): Someone hands the documents directly to the defendant. This is the most reliable method.

Alternatives to personal service:

  • For individuals: leave documents at the defendant's home with someone who appears to be an adult, then mail another copy
  • For corporations: leave documents at the corporation's registered office with someone who appears to be managing it
  • Mail to the last known address (certified mail with return receipt)
  • Courier delivery to the last known address

Who can serve?

Any person who is:

  • Not a party to the proceeding (not you, if you are the plaintiff)
  • At least 18 years of age

You can ask a friend, family member (not involved in the case), or professional process server.

Timing

You must serve the defendant within 6 months of the date the claim was issued by the court. If you cannot locate the defendant within this time, you can request an extension from the court.

Proof of service — Affidavit of Service (Form 8A)

The person who served the documents must complete an Affidavit of Service (Form 8A) swearing that service was completed. This sworn document must be filed with the court. You keep a copy for your records.

The affidavit must include: who was served, what was served, when and how service occurred, and the server's signature before a commissioner of oaths or notary public.

{# --- STAGE 4: Defendant's Response --- #}

The {{ defence_deadline_days }}-day deadline

After being served with the Plaintiff's Claim, the defendant has {{ defence_deadline_days }} days to respond. This deadline runs from the date of service (not the date of filing).

The defendant's options

1. File a Defence (Form 9A)

The defendant disputes the claim, either in whole or in part. The defence must:

  • Admit or deny each factual allegation
  • Give reasons for disputing the claim
  • Be filed at the court office with the {{ defence_filing_fee }} filing fee

If you are the defendant, generate your Form 9A using our tool →

2. File a Defence and Defendant's Claim (Form 10A)

The defendant disputes the claim AND is making a counterclaim against the plaintiff. This is done in the same proceeding.

3. Admit the claim and propose terms of payment

The defendant agrees they owe the money but asks to pay in installments. The plaintiff can accept or reject the proposed terms.

4. Do nothing

If the defendant does not file a defence within {{ defence_deadline_days }} days, the plaintiff can ask the court to "note the defendant in default." Once noted in default, the defendant cannot participate further without the court's permission. The plaintiff can then bring a motion for default judgment.

If no defence is filed (plaintiff perspective)

File a "Request to Clerk" form asking the clerk to note the defendant in default. Then file a motion for default judgment. The court will review your claim and may award judgment without a hearing.

{# --- STAGE 5: Settlement Conference --- #}

What is a settlement conference?

A settlement conference is a mandatory meeting with a judge or deputy judge before trial. It is designed to:

  • Help the parties explore whether they can settle without going to trial
  • Narrow and clarify the issues in dispute
  • Identify agreed facts
  • Discuss evidence and witnesses

You cannot go to trial in Small Claims Court without first attending a settlement conference.

When does it happen?

The court schedules the settlement conference after both sides have filed their documents. Timing varies by location and court availability — it may be weeks to several months after filing the defence.

Who attends?

All parties (or their representatives) must attend. If you have a lawyer or paralegal, they typically attend with you. You must attend in person (or via video if the court permits) — you cannot send someone else without permission.

What happens at the conference?

A judge presides. The conference is informal — there are no witnesses or formal evidence, just a discussion. The judge may:

  • Ask each side to explain their position briefly
  • Point out strengths and weaknesses in each side's case
  • Suggest a settlement range
  • Help draft a settlement agreement if the parties agree

Possible outcomes

  • Settlement: Both parties agree on terms — the case is over. Terms are recorded as a court order.
  • Partial settlement: Some issues resolved, remaining issues go to trial
  • No settlement: Case is scheduled for trial. The judge gives directions about evidence, witnesses, and timing.
  • Dismissal: If a party fails to appear or cooperate, the judge may dismiss the claim or defence
{# SETTLEMENT CONFERENCE PREPARATION SUB-GUIDE #}

Settlement Conference Preparation Guide

What to expect

The settlement conference is not a trial. You will not call witnesses or present evidence formally. However, the judge will want to understand your case, so be prepared to explain it clearly and concisely — in 5 to 10 minutes.

The judge may give you their frank assessment of your case's strengths and weaknesses. This is valuable feedback, even if it is not what you want to hear.

What to bring

  • All documents you intend to rely on (organized in a folder or binder)
  • A written summary of your position (1-2 pages)
  • A list of all witnesses you plan to call at trial
  • A list of the specific amounts you are claiming, with calculations
  • Any offers to settle you have already made or received
  • A copy of the Plaintiff's Claim and any Defence filed
  • Your calendar — you may need to agree on trial dates

How to present your case

  1. Be organized. Know the key facts and have them written down. Do not rely on memory alone.
  2. Be concise. Tell the judge the most important facts first. You will be asked to summarize, not give a full account.
  3. Focus on evidence. What can you prove? What documents or witnesses support your position?
  4. Know your best offer. Before the conference, decide how much you would accept to settle (or pay, if you are the defendant). You do not have to reveal this immediately, but knowing it helps you negotiate.
  5. Be respectful. Address the judge as "Your Honour." Speak calmly, even if the other side does not.

Dos and Don'ts

DoDon't
Arrive early — court delays are stressful Interrupt the other party or the judge
Listen carefully to what the judge says about your case Bring irrelevant evidence or air personal grievances
Come prepared with a realistic settlement offer Make ultimatums — keep negotiations open
Ask the judge to clarify anything you do not understand Refuse to engage in settlement discussions at all
Keep notes of what is discussed and decided Make statements that could be used against you at trial

Note: Statements made during a settlement conference are generally inadmissible at trial — the conference is confidential. However, any agreed terms recorded in a court order are binding.

After the conference

If you settled: keep your copy of the settlement order. If the other party does not comply, you can enforce it as a judgment.

If you did not settle: review the judge's directions carefully. Make note of any deadlines for disclosing evidence, serving witness lists, or filing additional documents.

You have 30 days after an unsuccessful settlement conference to request a trial date (Rule 13.05(2)).

{# --- STAGE 6: Trial Management Conference --- #}
New as of June 2025: Rule 16.1.01 of the Rules of the Small Claims Court introduced the Trial Management Conference (TMC) as a new mandatory step before trial. This was added to improve trial efficiency and reduce unnecessary delays.

What is a Trial Management Conference?

A Trial Management Conference (TMC) is a brief meeting with a judge that occurs after the settlement conference if the case is going to trial. It is designed to ensure both parties are ready and that the trial will run efficiently.

When does it happen?

The court schedules the TMC after the settlement conference has concluded without a full settlement and the case is proceeding to trial. The TMC typically occurs a few weeks before the scheduled trial date.

Purpose

At the TMC, the judge will:

  • Confirm the issues that remain in dispute
  • Review the witness lists from both sides
  • Estimate how long the trial will take
  • Confirm trial readiness — are documents disclosed? Are witnesses available?
  • Give any final directions to ensure the trial runs smoothly
  • Consider any last-minute settlement possibilities

What to bring to the TMC

  • Your complete witness list (name and what each witness will say)
  • Your complete document list (all exhibits you plan to use at trial)
  • Confirmation that all documents have been disclosed to the other side
  • A realistic estimate of how long your case will take to present

Failing to attend the TMC or being unprepared may result in the judge giving directions that disadvantage your case, or in extreme cases, dismissing the claim or striking the defence.

{# --- STAGE 7: Trial --- #}

How a Small Claims Court trial works

Small Claims Court trials are less formal than Superior Court trials, but they follow a structured process:

  1. Opening statements (optional): Each side may briefly explain what they will prove
  2. Plaintiff's case: The plaintiff presents their evidence and calls their witnesses
  3. Cross-examination: The defendant questions the plaintiff's witnesses
  4. Defendant's case: The defendant presents their evidence and calls their witnesses
  5. Cross-examination: The plaintiff questions the defendant's witnesses
  6. Closing arguments: Each side summarizes why they should win
  7. Judgment: The judge may give judgment immediately or "reserve" judgment (decide later)

Evidence rules

Small Claims Court has more relaxed evidence rules than Superior Court. Hearsay evidence (second-hand accounts) may be admitted, though the judge will weigh it accordingly. The court values practical fairness over strict procedural technicality.

Documents must generally be disclosed to the other side before trial — you cannot surprise the other party with new evidence at trial.

Witnesses

Witnesses testify under oath or affirmation. Both parties can question (examine) their own witnesses and cross-examine the other side's witnesses.

If a witness will not come voluntarily, you can issue a summons (a court order requiring attendance). There is a fee for issuing a summons, and the witness is entitled to attendance money and travel expenses.

Judgment

The judge considers the evidence and applies the law to decide who wins and how much (if anything) is awarded. The standard of proof is "balance of probabilities" — meaning it is more likely than not that the plaintiff's version is correct.

Costs

The winning party may be awarded representation costs (limited in Small Claims Court — usually capped at 15% of the amount claimed). The court can also award higher costs if a party behaved unreasonably.

Appealing a judgment

Either party can appeal a Small Claims Court judgment to the Divisional Court within 30 days of the judgment being made. Appeals are expensive and complex — you should consult a lawyer before appealing.

{# --- STAGE 8: After Judgment / Enforcement --- #}

If the other party pays voluntarily

Many parties pay after judgment without further action. Payment is typically expected within a reasonable time (often 30 days, or as specified in the judgment). If you receive full payment, file a satisfaction of judgment with the court.

If the other party does not pay

Winning a judgment does not automatically put money in your pocket — you may need to enforce it. Enforcement options include:

Garnishment: A court order directing a third party (typically an employer or bank) to pay money owed to the judgment debtor directly to you instead.

  • Wage garnishment: up to 20% of net wages (or as ordered)
  • Bank account garnishment: money in accounts up to the judgment amount

Writ of Seizure and Sale: A court order allowing the sheriff to seize and sell the debtor's personal property (not land in Small Claims Court). Fee: {{ '$55.00' }}.

Writ of Delivery: If your claim was for the return of specific property, this order allows the sheriff to seize and return the property. Fee: {{ '$55.00' }}.

Examination of Judgment Debtor: You can summon the debtor to court to answer questions under oath about their finances — their income, assets, bank accounts, and employer. This is an important step if you do not know where to direct enforcement.

Costs of enforcement

Enforcement costs (sheriff fees, filing fees for writs) can generally be added to the judgment amount and recovered from the debtor.

How long does a judgment last?

A Small Claims Court judgment is enforceable for 6 years from the date of the judgment. After 6 years, the judgment expires unless you take steps to renew it (by bringing a new action on the judgment). Post-judgment interest continues to accrue during this period at the rate set out in the judgment.

If you cannot collect

If the debtor has no assets or income ("judgment proof"), you may not be able to collect. This is a risk of litigation. Consider the debtor's ability to pay before filing a claim.
